01

Abstract

FandomForge is a prototype fanfiction platform designed for adolescents aged 13 to 17. The project examines whether a simpler, visually guided interface can improve motivation to read and write fanfiction while helping younger users locate relevant content more efficiently than existing platforms such as Archive of Our Own (AO3).

The design emphasizes reduced cognitive load, curated navigation, and an easier path into tags and recommendations. To evaluate the concept, the team conducted a comparative user study with 14 participants who completed content-discovery tasks on both AO3 and the FandomForge Figma prototype before responding to a Likert-scale questionnaire.

Across every major metric tied to beginner usability, FandomForge outperformed AO3. Motivation improved from 2.71 to 4.36 out of 5, exceeding the project’s 25% target, while participants also reported clearer navigation, better tag understanding, and substantially less cognitive overload.

02

Introduction

Digital fanfiction platforms operate as spaces for adolescent literacy, creativity, and community participation, but the current leaders place meaningful friction in front of younger users.

Leisure reading among adolescents has been declining, which makes the design of motivating, approachable reading environments more important. Existing fanfiction platforms already give teens access to stories, characters, and communities they care about, yet the entry experience remains uneven. AO3 offers powerful tagging and search, but its dense, text-heavy interface can overwhelm newer users. Wattpad emphasizes discovery and social activity, but often at the expense of filtering precision, readability, and user control.

FandomForge was designed to occupy the gap between those models. The prototype uses curated visual navigation and a simplified discovery flow to support adolescents who want choice and relevance without needing deep platform literacy on day one.

Research Snapshot

What the study set out to prove

Research question
To what extent does FandomForge improve adolescents’ perceived motivation and efficiency in finding relevant fanfiction content compared to AO3?
Hypothesis
A reduced, curated interface guided by Hick’s Law would increase motivation scores by at least 25% while reducing overload and improving task success.
Audience
Adolescents aged 13 to 17, especially beginners and low-experience users.
Design stance
Balance simplicity and discovery with enough user control to stay useful.

03

Related Work

The literature and platform review converge on the same issue: adolescent motivation increases when platforms offer relevance, social connection, and clear discovery paths, but current fanfiction systems often require too much interface literacy from beginners.

Published Research · 2005

Black, “Access and Affiliation”

Rebecca W. Black found that online fanfiction communities motivate adolescents to write because they combine authentic audiences with strong social feedback loops.

Why it matters: Motivation grows when young users can reach community quickly.

What FandomForge adds: It focuses on the interface conditions that help beginners access those community benefits in the first place.

Published Research · 2017

Lammers and Palumbo, “Barriers to Fanfiction Access”

Their usability inspection of FanFiction.net found that navigation, reading flow, and social participation break down for screen-reader users because of cluttered structure and erratic page behavior.

Why it matters: Poor interface structure can prevent access rather than merely slow it down.

What FandomForge adds: It extends the concern from disability access to the broader beginner experience for adolescent users.

Published Research · 2023

Webber et al., “Supporting Adolescents’ Reading Motivation”

Across 38 peer-reviewed studies, the most effective interventions were the ones that gave teenagers meaningful choice, community, and personal relevance.

Why it matters: Motivation design matters more than adding academic structure alone.

What FandomForge adds: It applies those principles in an out-of-school, fandom-driven reading context the review identified as underexplored.

Published Research · 2024

Silberstein-Bamford, “Thank God for Tags”

This study shows that AO3 readers use tags as a way to seek precise, on-demand reading experiences, often following tropes or authors across fandom boundaries.

Why it matters: Discoverability is central to fanfiction reading behavior.

What FandomForge adds: It aims to preserve the value of precise discovery without requiring experienced-community tag literacy.

Published Research · 2022

Cheng and Frens, “Feedback Exchange and Online Affinity”

Feedback relationships form more easily when writers find authentic social connection, but newcomers still face integration barriers when entering the platform and the community around it.

Why it matters: Onboarding shapes whether users ever reach the rewarding part of the community experience.

What FandomForge adds: It focuses on reducing those barriers earlier through clearer discovery and orientation.

04

Other Commercial Systems

FandomForge was positioned against the two reference platforms most relevant to the study: AO3 for depth and tagging precision, and FanFiction.net for legacy popularity and long-standing usability issues.

Reference Platform

Archive of Our Own (AO3)

AO3 is one of fanfiction’s most prominent platforms because it offers flexible tagging, detailed filtering, and deep content coverage. That same power creates a high cognitive barrier for beginners.

  • Powerful tags, but many are opaque without community knowledge.
  • Text-heavy lists make scanning slower and reduce visual hierarchy.
  • Long fandom indexes and dense metadata create friction for adolescents.
  • Moderation limits make the platform inappropriate for younger audiences.

Reference Platform

FanFiction.net

FanFiction.net remains historically important, but the report positions it as a cautionary example of cluttered interface design, weak visual hierarchy, and poor moderation outcomes.

  • Reading and discovery rely on crowded, low-priority text blocks.
  • Navigation lacks visual structure for fast content triage.
  • Community trust has been hurt by bot activity and moderation failures.
  • The platform is not a safe fit for the project’s adolescent audience.
